Modules 2–3 · The Inanimate World

The Forces That
Make It Possible

The physical conditions that make pole dancing possible were fixed at the Big Bang, 13.8 billion years ago, and have not changed since.1

The six fundamental constants acting on a dancer's body were fixed at the very beginning of the universe, and if any one were even slightly different, the cosmos would be unrecognisable.2 Two of these are directly visible in every pole session. Pole dancing is a demonstration of these two forces beautifully landing in their Goldilocks zone.

1036
Ratio of electromagnetism to gravity - the relationship that makes pole dancing physically possible3
Gravity
Fundamental Constant

Gives us weight and makes every hold and inversion a fight. Its specific value sits in a narrow window: strong enough that bodyweight is meaningful resistance to work against, weak enough that a human body can overcome it temporarily.4

If stronger →
Most moves become impossible. Bodyweight pulled down would exceed what human muscles can generate.
If weaker →
The move loses its meaning. You'd float up with minimal effort. The entire logic of the sport dissolves.

The climb and the aerial invert both make gravity visible. In the climb, every upward movement is a direct contest with bodyweight - the effort is the point. In the aerial invert, the body temporarily defies the pull entirely, suspended above its own centre of gravity. Neither move would exist as a meaningful feat under different gravitational conditions.

Electrical Force
Fundamental Constant

The force behind friction - the grip between skin and pole is electromagnetic attraction between atoms at the surface of two materials.5 Almost everything tactile in pole dancing is electromagnetism.

If stronger →
Skin grip locks you to the pole. Releasing mid-move becomes dangerous. Most moves stiffen into immobility.
If weaker →
Grip disappears entirely. Every contact point fails. You'd need mechanical grip for everything.
jade
marley

The jade and marley are the clearest examples - moves where skin contact alone is the only thing keeping you up, with no additional grip points. That contact works within a specific range - enough friction to hold bodyweight, not so much that movement becomes impossible.

Modules 4–6 · The History of Life

Why The Body
Wants To Do This

Evolution explains not only how we are able to do pole tricks, but also why we might even want to.

It is all an accumulation of solutions to completely different problems over hundreds of millions of years. The body pole dancing uses was not built for pole dancing - it was built for survival, and pole dancing just happens to activate all of it at once.

~500 million years ago
Proprioception
Directed movement and body awareness evolved in the earliest motile single-celled organisms - a sensor detecting food or danger, connected to a tail. The bedrock of every pole trick.6
~400 million years ago
Load-Bearing Limbs
When vertebrates moved onto land, bodies had to hold their own weight against gravity for the first time.7 The bilateral body plan, spine, and paired limbs - the architecture pole dancing still runs on.
~200 million years ago
The Mammalian Brain
A more sophisticated neuromuscular system and finer motor control. The dopamine reward system for skill acquisition is an extension of this - the brain learned to make useful behaviours feel good.8
~60 million years ago
Primates
Tree-dwelling survival selected for exactly what pole dancing demands: grip, shoulder rotation, bearing bodyweight overhead, inverting, hanging. The pole dancer's body is a refined version of this.
Today
You, In A Studio
Bipedalism made grip strength redundant - but evolution doesn't reverse course. Path dependency locked the hardware in.9 The same circuits that rewarded tree-climbing fire when you nail a trick.

"Play exists to make practicing survival skills feel rewarding. The enjoyment is not incidental - it is the mechanism."

The Gaia parallel: just as life creates and maintains the conditions for its own survival through feedback loops, play reinforces the physical behaviours the organism needs to survive by making them feel good.10

Pole dancing activates a very old reward circuit doing exactly what it evolved to do - just in a context it never anticipated. It is one continuous line from the first single-celled organism that learned to move toward food, to the primate brain that learned to make climbing feel good, to a human choosing to spend an evening in a pole studio for the fun of it.

Modules 9–10 · Human History

Where Pole Dancing
Actually Came From

Tracing the real origins - erotic performance, race, capitalism, and the women who built the sport.

Pole-related traditions appeared independently across many cultures - the European Maypole, Indian Mallakhamb, Chinese pole acrobatics. While these are sometimes listed as the origins of the sport, there is no real evidence connecting any of these to modern pole dancing.11 Likely, they are brought up as an effort to distance the sport from its real roots.

The real origins are Middle-Eastern and Romani women performing in the travelling circus in the late 19th century USA. Not formally paid - they relied on tips, which likely contributed to the show becoming more enticing. They used the pole in the centre of the tent for support while removing clothing, and to climb and spin around it.12

When these women of colour started integrating into burlesque - previously strictly whites-only - they were promoted as "exotics" with descriptors like "jungle fever." The term "exotic dancer" emerged to distance them from white performers portrayed as more sophisticated.13 The hypersexualisation that became attached to pole dancing, and the erasure of who actually built it, both have roots here.

In agrarian societies, women were expected to focus their lives on bearing children, which tied them to the home.15 For women in most of recorded history, the body was one of the very few economic resources they had direct control over - which helps explain why entertainers was one of the first ever professions, recorded in the Uruk Standard Professions List.14

By the 1980s, the specific vocabulary of modern pole dancing developed inside strip clubs11 - dancers innovating within constrained circumstances, building techniques that are still the foundation of the sport today.

Modules 11–12 · The Anthropocene

A Sport On
Our Own Terms

How demographic, economic, and gender shifts during the Anthropocene produced a new chapter for pole dancing.

Although pole dancing is still often related to the context of erotic performance, it now also exists as a form of sport and a hobby that has nothing to do with it apart from its history. This new chapter can be traced back to specific historical changes during the Anthropocene.

As economies industrialised, child mortality dropped dramatically - families no longer needed to have many children to ensure some survived.16 Children shifted from being economic assets to economic costs. The rational response was to have fewer children and invest more in each one.

Fewer pregnancies meant more time, more energy, and eventually more economic independence. The commercial pole dancing industry as it exists today is part of the consumerist economy dependent on a growing global middle class with purchasing power - and especially women's entry into that class as independent earners.16

The shift from pole dancing as erotic performance staged for a male audience to a sport practiced on our own terms was only possible due to legal and social shifts around gender that accelerated through the twentieth century - access to education, reproductive rights, legal protections, and changing norms around work and public life.17

While the sport is still dominated by women, other genders are increasingly represented - male participation grew by over 70% when male categories were opened in 2017.18 The sport still remains political. There are ongoing debates about whether it belongs in the Olympics, whether it is empowering or exploitative, and whether its erotic history should be celebrated or distanced from.

Pole dancing today - on your own terms

"I get to choose what pole dancing means for me - how sexual it is, who gets to see it and in what context."

While I take that choice for granted in my everyday life, learning about its history and seeing all the discourse around pole dancing reminds me how remarkable this actually is - and how it is the result of changes that took a very long time to happen and that are still not complete.

Having said that, this is not why I started the hobby or what drives me to the studio every week. At the end of the day, this is simply a fun and enjoyable activity that my body and brain have evolved to love.
