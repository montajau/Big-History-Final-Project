Modules 2–3 · The Inanimate World

The Forces That Make It Possible

The physical conditions that make pole dancing possible are the endpoint of 13.8 billion years of cosmic history. The six fundamental constants acting on a dancer's body were fixed at the very beginning of the universe, and if any one were even slightly different, the cosmos would be unrecognisable.

Two of these are directly visible in every pole session. The ratio of electromagnetism to gravity alone is 10³⁶ — and it is precisely this relationship that makes pole dancing physically possible. Pole dancing could be viewed as a demonstration of these two forces beautifully landing in the Goldilocks zone.

Ratio of electromagnetism to gravity

Module 2 · Fundamental Constant
Gravity

Gives us weight and makes every hold and inversion a fight. Strong enough that bodyweight is meaningful resistance to work against — weak enough that a human body can overcome it temporarily.

If stronger →

Most moves become impossible. The bodyweight pulled down exceeds what human muscles can generate.

If weaker →

The move loses its meaning. You'd float up with minimal effort. The entire logic of the sport dissolves.

Module 2 · Fundamental Constant
Electrical Force

The force behind friction — the grip between skin and pole is electromagnetic attraction between atoms at the surface of two materials. Almost everything tactile in pole dancing is electromagnetism.

If stronger →

The skin grip locks you to the pole. Releasing mid-move becomes dangerous. The jade and genie stiffen into immobility.

If weaker →

The grip disappears entirely. Every contact point fails. You'd need mechanical grip for everything — a completely different sport.

Modules 4–6 · The History of Life

Why The Body Wants To Do This

The lens of evolution can be used not only to explain how we are able to do pole tricks, but also why we might even want to. It is all an accumulation of solutions to completely different problems over hundreds of millions of years.

~500 million years ago
Proprioception
Directed movement and the sense of body position evolved in the earliest motile single-celled organisms — a sensor detecting food or danger, connected to a tail that moved toward or away from it. The absolute bedrock of every pole trick.
~400 million years ago
Load-Bearing Limbs
When vertebrates moved onto land, bodies had to hold their own weight against gravity for the first time. Bilateral body plan, spine, paired limbs — the architecture pole dancing still runs on.
~200 million years ago
The Mammalian Brain
A more sophisticated neuromuscular system, finer motor control. The brain evolved as an image-forming system where any change that improved survival was reinforced. The dopamine reward system is an extension of this — the brain learned to make useful behaviours feel good.
~60 million years ago
Primates
Tree-dwelling survival selected for exactly what pole dancing demands: grip, shoulder rotation, bearing bodyweight overhead, inverting, hanging. The pole dancer's body is a refined version of this, built by millions of years of selection pressure.
Today
You, In A Studio
Bipedalism made grip strength redundant for survival — but evolution doesn't reverse course. Path dependency locked the hardware in. The same circuits that once rewarded tree-climbing fire when you nail a trick. The brain doesn't know the context changed.
"Play exists to make practicing survival skills feel rewarding. The enjoyment is not incidental — it is the mechanism."

The Gaia parallel: just as life creates and maintains the conditions for its own survival through feedback loops, play reinforces the physical behaviours the organism needs to survive by making them feel good. Pole dancing activates a very old reward circuit doing exactly what it evolved to do — just in a context it never anticipated.

This is why pole dancing feels the way it does in a way that's genuinely hard to explain. It is one continuous line from the first single-celled organism that learned to move toward food, to the primate brain that learned to make climbing feel good, to a human choosing to spend an evening in a pole studio for the fun of it.

Modules 9–10 · Human History

Where Pole Dancing Actually Came From

Pole-related traditions appeared independently across many cultures — the European Maypole, Indian Mallakhamb, Chinese pole acrobatics. While these are sometimes listed as the origins of the sport, there is no real evidence connecting any of these to modern pole dancing. Likely, they are brought up as an effort to distance it from its real roots.

The real origins are Middle-Eastern and Romani women performing in the travelling circus in the late 19th century USA. These women were not formally paid — they relied on tips from the crowd, which likely contributed to the show becoming more and more enticing. They used the pole in the centre of the tent for support while removing clothing, and to climb and spin around it.

When these women of colour started integrating into burlesque — previously strictly whites-only — they were promoted as "exotics" with descriptors like "jungle fever" or "voodoo mistress." The term "exotic dancer" emerged to distance them from white performers portrayed as more high-class and sophisticated.

The hypersexualisation that became attached to pole dancing, and the erasure of who actually built it, both have roots here.

In agrarian societies, women were expected to focus their lives on bearing children, which tied them to the home. The shift to industrialisation did not change that. For women in most of recorded history, the body was one of the very few economic resources they had direct control over — which helps explain why entertainers was one of the first ever professions, recorded in the Uruk Standard Professions List.

By the 1980s, the specific vocabulary of modern pole dancing developed inside strip clubs — dancers innovating within constrained economic circumstances, building the techniques that are still the foundation of the sport today.

Module 11–12 · The Anthropocene

A Sport On Our Own Terms

Although pole dancing is still often related to the context of erotic performance, it now also exists as a form of sport and a hobby that has nothing to do with it apart from its history. This new chapter can be traced back to specific historical changes during the Anthropocene.

As economies industrialised, child mortality dropped dramatically — families no longer needed to have many children to ensure some survived. Children shifted from being economic assets to economic costs. The rational response was to have fewer children and invest more in each one.

Fewer pregnancies meant more time, more energy, and eventually more economic independence. The commercial pole dancing industry as it exists today is part of the consumerist economy dependent on a growing global middle class with purchasing power — and especially women's entry into that class as independent earners.

The shift from pole dancing as erotic performance staged for a male audience to a sport practiced on our own terms was only possible due to the legal and social shifts around gender that accelerated through the twentieth century — access to education, reproductive rights, legal protections, and changing norms around work and public life.

The sport still remains political. There are ongoing debates about whether it belongs in the Olympics, whether it is empowering or exploitative, whether its erotic history should be celebrated or distanced from.
